Database Story for Curious Builders

Punch cards, magnetic disks, relational theory, lakehouses—follow how we learned to trust databases with the world’s records

In 1890 the U.S. Census Bureau rushed to finish tabulations with Hollerith punch cards. By the 1960s rocket factories relied on IMS to trace every bolt. Researchers asked, “What if every dataset was a table?” and relational theory took root.

Walk from Codd’s relational paper through Peter Chen’s ER diagrams, Oracle’s first SQL release, the rise of PostgreSQL and MongoDB, and today’s cloud-spanning systems like Spanner. Finish with lakehouse blueprints and vector databases built for AI copilots.

1890s

Automation takes its first census steps

Punch cards and electromechanical tabulators counted what paper clerks could not, proving machines could handle official records.

1950s

Magnetic disks and file design

Random-access storage arrived just as enterprises argued about how to structure records and links across sprawling programs.

1960s

Enterprise data models under pressure

Space programs and banks needed to trace every part and account, inspiring hierarchical and network database designs.

1970s

Relational ideas and languages

Mathematicians and practitioners proposed tables, relationships, and SQL so developers could declare intent instead of navigating pointers.

1980s

Standards and high-performance systems

Banks and manufacturers adopted relational databases at scale, pushing for SQL standards and massively parallel hardware.

1990s

The web, open source, and data warehouses

Web services and ecommerce demanded affordable databases and fresh analytics, paving the way for MySQL, PostgreSQL, and warehouse blueprints.

2000s

Big data and distributed storage

Internet giants processed massive logs with MapReduce and Dynamo-inspired architectures, while document-oriented NoSQL gained traction.

2010s

Cloud-native consistency and streaming

Globally distributed services chased strong guarantees and fast pipelines, mixing ACID transactions with real-time event streams.

2020s

Lakehouses and vector search

Hybrid lakehouse designs and vector databases emerged to power analytics and AI copilots from the same trusted store.
